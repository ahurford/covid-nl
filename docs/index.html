COVID-19 in Newfoundland and Labrador

Amy Hurford, Zahra Mohammadi, and Francis Anokye

Memorial University of Newfoundland and Labrador, University of Guelph, Canadian Network for Modelling Infectious Disease, Mathematics for Public Health, One Health Modelling Network for Emerging Infections

On this website we:

  • visualize COVID-19 cases reported by Newfoundland and Labrador (NL);

  • estimate the doubling time of reported cases; and

  • predict hospitalizations and deaths.

The data begins on December 15, 2021, when the first Omicron case was reported in NL.

Testing

Recommendations for PCR testing changed in NL on January 3rd, 2022, such that anyone that is a close contact of a case and has symptoms of COVID-19, should consider themselves a positive case and not schedule a PCR test. In British Columbia (BC), people under 65 are discouraged from seeking PCR testing. The BC Modelling Group estimates that the number of cases reported in BC would be 3.6 times higher without this change in testing.

To assess the impact of the change in PCR testing recommendations, we assumed that the number of tests reported for NL on a given day is the sum of two components:

  1. \(a_1\) or \(a_2\): the number of tests arising due to a contact with a COVID-19 case reported 3 days earlier, and
  2. \(b\): the number of tests performed per person in NL;

where \(a_1\) is prior to January 6, and \(a_2\) is after January 6th. We assume a 3 day lag between testing and reporting, such that if on January 3 symptomatic contacts of a case did not get tested the effect would first appear in the reporting on January 6. Using the data from the Public Service Annoucements we estimate that:

\(a_1=\) 2.02 tests per case 3 days prior (to January 3)

\(a_2=\) 1.27 tests per case 3 days prior (after January 3)

\(b=\) 0.01 tests per person in NL.

Correcting for the PCR testing changes and out-of-province test results

To correct for the change in PCR testing recommendations (beginning January 3), we assume that beginning January 6th the reporting reflects 0.75 fewer tests per case, and that all these test that did not occur would have been positive. We use this correction so that reported cases can be compared before and after January 6.

On January 10, 2022, NL reported 680 cases arising from tests sent out of province December 29 to January 6th. The numbers from the Public Service Announcement suggest that all 680 cases were in Eastern Health. To correct the case data these 680 cases are distributed in Eastern Health in proportion to the number of reported cases on each day December 29 to January 6th. The addition of these cases is after the correction for the PCR testing change.

All subsequent calculations are performed on the adjusted data.

Doubling and halving times

The doubling time is the days until the number of reported cases doubles (assuming exponential growth). The halving time is the days until the number of reported cases halves (assuming exponential decay). Our doubling and halving time calculations consider the number of reported cases over the last 7 days. If the exponential growth coefficient is not different from 0, the number of reported cases is stated as ‘At peak’.

Hospitalizations and deaths

We consider data from United Kingdom Health Security Agency (UKHSA) and Public Health Ontario (PHO) reports to understand the relationship between reported Omicron cases and the future number of hospitalizations and deaths.

The UKHSA study reports for confirmed Omicron cases: 198,348 cases, 815 hospital admissions (0.41%), and 57 deaths (0.03%) up until December 29, 2021. The PHO study reports for Omicron: 29,594 cases, 75 hospitalizations or deaths (0.25%), and 3 deaths (0.01%) from November 22 - December 17, 2021.
